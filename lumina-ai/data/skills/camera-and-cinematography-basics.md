# Camera & Cinematography Basics

Cinematography is the craft of **visual storytelling through the camera** — what's in frame, from what angle, how it moves. Understanding it helps you compose real footage *and* write effective prompts for AI video generation (where naming shot types, angles, and movements steers the output — see prompt-engineering-for-visual-media, how-video-generation-works).

## Shot Sizes (how much you show)

Distance from subject conveys different things:
- **Wide / establishing shot** — shows the whole scene/environment; sets context and location.
- **Medium shot** — subject from roughly the waist up; natural for dialogue and interaction.
- **Close-up** — a face or detail, fills the frame; conveys **emotion** and intimacy, directs attention.
- **Extreme close-up** — a single detail (eyes, an object) for emphasis/tension.
- **Long/extreme-long** — subject small in a vast setting; scale, isolation.
Shot size is a storytelling choice: closer = more emotional/intimate, wider = more context/scale.

## Camera Angles

The angle shapes how we perceive the subject:
- **Eye-level** — neutral, natural.
- **Low angle** (looking up) — makes the subject **powerful/dominant/large**.
- **High angle** (looking down) — makes them **small/weak/vulnerable**.
- **Dutch/canted** (tilted) — unease, disorientation, tension.
- **Over-the-shoulder, POV** — perspective and relationship.

## Camera Movement

Motion adds energy and guides attention:
- **Pan** (horizontal pivot) / **Tilt** (vertical pivot) — sweep across or up/down a scene.
- **Dolly / tracking** (camera physically moves) — follow a subject, reveal space (feels immersive).
- **Zoom** (lens, not camera) — moves closer optically (different feel from a dolly).
- **Crane / aerial** — sweeping vertical/overhead moves; grandeur.
- **Handheld** — shaky, immediate, documentary/tense feel; vs **static/tripod** — stable, formal.
Each movement has an emotional connotation — naming the right one (in a shoot or an AI prompt) shapes the feel.

## Composition

How elements are arranged in the frame (see visual-emphasis-and-hierarchy, gestalt-ui-organisation):
- **Rule of thirds** — place key elements along thirds/intersections, not dead center — more dynamic, balanced.
- **Leading lines** — lines that draw the eye toward the subject.
- **Headroom & look room** — appropriate space above the head and in the direction a subject faces/moves.
- **Framing & balance** — use foreground/background, symmetry or deliberate asymmetry.
- **Depth** — foreground/midground/background layers create dimensionality.

## Depth of Field

How much is in focus: **shallow** depth of field (blurry background, sharp subject) isolates the subject and looks "cinematic"; **deep** focus keeps everything sharp (context, scale). Controlled by aperture/lens (see how-digital-cameras-work). A key expressive tool.

## Pitfalls (in understanding/using)

- **Same shot size/angle** throughout → monotonous; vary shots to tell the story and hold interest.
- **Ignoring composition** (dead-center everything, no headroom, cluttered frames) → amateur, unbalanced look.
- **Movement for its own sake** — gratuitous pans/zooms distract; every move should have a purpose.
- Wrong **angle/size** for the emotion (a wide shot for an intimate emotional beat).
- For **AI video prompts**: not specifying shot type/angle/movement → generic framing; name them to control output (see prompt-engineering-for-visual-media).
- Forgetting **depth of field** as an expressive tool (flat, everything-in-focus look when isolation is wanted).
- Breaking continuity/eyelines across shots (jarring — see storyboarding-and-shot-planning).
