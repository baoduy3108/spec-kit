# Habit Formation

Habits are behaviors that become automatic — which is powerful, because willpower is limited but habits run on autopilot. Understanding the loop lets you engineer them.

## The Habit Loop

Every habit is **Cue → Routine → Reward**: a trigger prompts a behavior that delivers a payoff, and the brain learns to repeat it. To **build** a habit, design all three; to **break** one, disrupt the loop (usually by removing/avoiding the cue or replacing the routine with a better one that gives a similar reward).

## Make Good Habits Easy (and bad ones hard)

Behavior follows the path of least resistance, so **design your environment**:
- **Obvious** — make the cue visible (gym clothes by the bed, the book on your pillow). Hide the cues for bad habits (junk food out of sight, app off the home screen).
- **Easy** — reduce friction (prep in advance, lower the starting effort). Add friction to bad habits (log out, delete the app, unplug).
- **Satisfying** — reward it immediately, even small (tick a tracker, feel the win). Make bad habits immediately unsatisfying/costly.

## Start Tiny

The biggest reason habits fail is starting too big. Begin with a **ridiculously small** version — "two push-ups," "read one page," "meditate one minute." Tiny is sustainable, builds the *identity* ("I'm someone who exercises") and the routine; you scale up once the behavior is automatic. Consistency of a small action beats occasional intensity.

## Techniques

- **Habit stacking** — anchor a new habit to an existing one: "after I pour my morning coffee, I'll write for five minutes." The established habit is the cue.
- **Implementation intentions** — decide *when and where* in advance ("I will run at 7am in the park"), not just "I'll run more." Specificity dramatically raises follow-through.
- **Identity-based** — focus on *who you want to become*, not just the goal ("be a runner," not "run a marathon"); each action is a vote for that identity.
- **Track it** — a visible streak/log is motivating (don't break the chain).

## Consistency Over Perfection

Missing once is fine; **never miss twice**. One lapse doesn't undo progress — but two in a row starts a new (bad) pattern. Expect slips, plan to restart immediately, and don't let "I broke my streak" become "so I quit." Long-term change is showing up repeatedly, not being perfect.

## Pitfalls

- **Too big, too fast** → unsustainable, then abandoned.
- **Relying on motivation/willpower** instead of design — motivation fluctuates; systems don't.
- **No clear cue** — vague intentions ("more often") don't trigger.
- **All-or-nothing** — quitting after one missed day.
- Not changing the **environment** — fighting cues with willpower loses.
- Chasing intensity over **consistency** — the daily small action compounds.
