# Conflict Resolution

Conflict is normal and, handled well, can strengthen a team by surfacing real issues. Handled badly, it festers. The goal isn't to "win" — it's a resolution both sides can accept while preserving the relationship.

## Address It Early and Privately

Small tensions ignored grow into big ones. Raise issues **early**, before resentment builds, and **privately** — never criticize someone in front of others (it triggers defensiveness and humiliation). Pick a calm moment, not the heat of the argument.

## Separate the People From the Problem

Attack the **problem**, not the person (the same principle as feedback and negotiation). "The deadline slipped and it hurt the launch" is a shared problem to solve; "you're irresponsible" is an attack that guarantees a fight. Frame it as "us vs the problem," not "me vs you." Assume good intent — most conflict is misunderstanding or competing pressures, not malice.

## Understand the Underlying Interests

Positions clash ("we must do it my way"); the **interests** behind them often don't (see negotiation-basics). Dig for *why* each side wants what they want — the real need, fear, or constraint. Two people fighting over one resource might both be satisfied a different way once you understand what each actually needs. Ask questions; get curious before you get defensive.

## Listen Actively

The single most powerful move: genuinely **listen** and make the other person feel **heard**. Let them finish without interrupting; reflect back what you heard ("so you're worried that…") to confirm understanding. People de-escalate dramatically when they feel understood — and you often discover the conflict was smaller or different than you thought. You can't resolve what you don't understand.

## Find Common Ground and Resolve

- Identify what you **agree** on (usually the shared goal) and build from there.
- Look for **win-win** or fair compromise; involve both sides in the solution so they own it.
- Focus on the **future** ("how do we prevent this / move forward?") rather than relitigating the past and assigning blame.
- Agree on **specific next steps** and, if it recurs, revisit.

## When It's Heated

If emotions are too high, **take a break** — nothing productive happens while people are flooded. Stay calm; don't match escalation. For serious or stuck conflicts, a neutral **mediator** (manager, third party) helps.

## Pitfalls

- **Avoiding it** — hoping it resolves itself; it usually worsens.
- **Making it personal** / attacking character instead of the problem.
- **Public confrontation** → humiliation and entrenchment.
- **Arguing positions** without exploring interests → deadlock.
- **Not listening** — waiting to talk instead of understanding.
- **Relitigating the past** / blame instead of solving forward.
- Trying to resolve while both parties are still furious.
