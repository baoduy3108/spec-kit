# User Research

Good research reduces the risk of building the wrong thing. Bad research (leading questions, hypotheticals) produces confident, wrong conclusions — worse than none.

## Pick the Method for the Question

- **Interviews** — understand problems, motivations, context (the *why*). Qualitative, small n.
- **Usability testing** — watch someone *use* the product to find where they struggle. 5 users surface most major issues.
- **Surveys** — quantify something you already understand qualitatively; measure at scale. Bad for discovery.
- **Analytics / behavioral data** — what people actually do at scale (the *what*), no self-report bias. Pair with qualitative for the *why*.
Match method to whether you need depth (qual) or breadth (quant), and *why* vs *what*.

## Asking Questions Without Leading

- **Ask about past behavior, not hypothetical futures.** "What did you do last time you needed X?" beats "Would you use a feature that…?" — people are terrible at predicting their own future behavior and will say yes to be nice.
- **Non-leading, open-ended** — "Tell me about…" not "Don't you find X frustrating?" A leading question manufactures the answer you wanted.
- **Dig for the story and the why** — "Walk me through that", "Why did that matter?", then stay silent and let them talk.
- **Separate problem from solution** — validate the problem exists and hurts before asking about any solution; don't pitch.

## Usability Testing

Give a realistic task and **shut up** — don't coach, hint, or explain. Ask them to think aloud. When they get stuck, that's the finding — resist rescuing them. You're testing the design, not the person; their struggle is data, not failure. Watch what they *do*, weight it over what they *say*.

## Synthesize Into Decisions

- Look for **patterns across participants**, not one loud anecdote. A single quote isn't a trend.
- Separate observations (what happened) from interpretations (what it means) — and note how many people showed each.
- Beware confirmation bias — actively look for evidence against your favored idea.
- End with **decisions/actions**, not a report nobody reads: what you'll change, what to test next.

## Watch For

Small samples over-generalized; selection bias (you interviewed only fans); social-desirability bias (people tell you what sounds good); and treating stated intent as behavior. Triangulate qual + quant when the stakes are high.
