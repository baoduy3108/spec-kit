# Delegation

Delegation is how you scale beyond your own hours and develop other people. Most people delegate too little (bottlenecking everything through themselves) or badly (dumping tasks with no context, then micromanaging).

## Why Delegate

You can't do everything, and hoarding work makes you the bottleneck while your team stays junior. Delegation frees you for higher-leverage work **and** grows the people you delegate to — stretch assignments are how people develop. "I can do it faster myself" is true short-term and a trap long-term.

## Delegate Outcomes, Not Just Tasks

Hand over the **goal and the authority** to achieve it, not a rote checklist. "Own getting the newsletter shipped weekly" develops ownership; "do exactly these 5 steps I dictate" develops nothing and keeps you in the loop forever. Say **what** success looks like and **why** it matters — then let them figure out the **how** (their way may differ from yours and still be fine, or better).

## Communicate What Matters

- **Context** — why this matters, how it fits the bigger picture, constraints, and any hard boundaries (budget, deadline, things not to touch).
- **Success criteria** — how you'll both know it's done well (specific, observable).
- **Resources and authority** — what they can decide/spend/use without asking, and whom to go to.
- **Deadline and check-in points.**
Vague handoffs ("handle the client thing") set people up to fail.

## Match the Person and the Oversight

Delegate to someone with (or ready to grow into) the capability. Then **match oversight to their experience and the task's risk**: a proven person on a low-risk task needs a deadline and "come to me if stuck"; a newer person or a high-stakes task needs more check-ins and earlier feedback. Agree the **check-in cadence** up front so it's support, not surveillance.

## Then Let Go

Once delegated, **resist micromanaging** and resist **taking it back** at the first wobble. Let them do it their way; offer help when asked or at check-ins; accept "good enough / done differently" over "exactly how I'd do it." Taking the task back the moment it's imperfect teaches people that delegation is fake and that you'll always rescue them — so they stop owning anything. Support, coach, but leave the responsibility with them.

## Pitfalls

- **Not delegating** — hoarding work, becoming the bottleneck, burning out.
- **Dumping** — handing off with no context, criteria, or authority.
- **Micromanaging** — delegating the task but not the decisions.
- **Taking it back** at the first imperfection → kills ownership.
- **Delegating only drudgery** and never growth opportunities → demotivating.
- **Mismatched oversight** — abandoning a beginner, or hovering over an expert.
- Delegating **responsibility without authority** — they're accountable but can't decide anything.
