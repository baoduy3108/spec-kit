# Color Grading Basics

Color grading is adjusting the **color and tone** of video/images — both to **fix** technical issues and to create a **look/mood**. It's a major part of why professional footage looks "cinematic" and amateur footage doesn't, and it's a key finishing step for real or AI-generated media (see color-expert, color-science). *(Applies to photo and video.)*

## Correction vs Creative Grading

Two distinct stages:
- **Color correction** — **fixing** the footage to look correct/natural: proper **exposure** (not too dark/bright), accurate **white balance** (whites look white, no unwanted color cast), and balancing shots so they match technically. The neutral, "make it right" pass.
- **Creative grading** — **stylizing** for mood and story: warm golden tones for nostalgia/comfort, cool blue for cold/tense/sad, teal-and-orange blockbuster looks, desaturated for grit, high-contrast for drama. This is color as **storytelling** — deliberately shaping emotion.
Correct first (get a clean, neutral base), then grade creatively.

## The Tools

- **White balance** — set the color temperature so the image isn't too orange (warm) or blue (cool). The foundational correction.
- **Exposure / contrast** — brightness and the range from shadows to highlights.
- **Color wheels — lift, gamma, gain** — adjust color in the **shadows** (lift), **midtones** (gamma), and **highlights** (gain) separately. This lets you, e.g., add warmth to highlights and coolness to shadows (a classic cinematic split-tone).
- **Saturation / vibrance** — intensity of color.
- **Curves & HSL** — fine control over tone and specific hues.
- **Scopes** (waveform, vectorscope) — read the image objectively, since your eyes/monitor deceive you.

## LUTs

A **LUT (Look-Up Table)** is a preset color transformation — a saved "look" you apply to footage. LUTs speed up grading (apply a base look instantly) and ensure consistency, and technical LUTs convert log/flat footage to a standard look. Useful, but a LUT is a **starting point**, not a magic fix — it still needs adjustment per shot (a LUT slapped on badly-exposed footage looks bad).

## Consistency Across Shots

A crucial goal: shots in a sequence should **match** — consistent color and tone so cuts feel seamless (see video-editing-fundamentals). Footage from different cameras, lighting, or (for AI) different generations can vary; grading **unifies** them into one coherent look. Inconsistent color between shots is jarring and amateur. (For AI-generated clips with varying color, grading to a common look is essential — see generative-media-pipeline.)

## Color as Mood

Color powerfully shapes emotion (see color-expert, color-science): warm vs cool, saturated vs muted, high vs low contrast all carry feeling. A consistent **color palette/look** also builds identity and atmosphere (see brand-visual-language). Grade intentionally toward the mood the story wants.

## Pitfalls (in understanding/using)

- **Skipping correction** — creative grading on unbalanced/wrong-exposed footage looks bad; fix the base first.
- **Overgrading** — pushing color/contrast too far (crushed shadows, blown highlights, garish tints) — subtle usually looks better.
- **Inconsistent shots** — color that jumps between cuts; match shots for a seamless sequence.
- Relying on a **LUT** as a one-click fix without per-shot adjustment.
- **Trusting your eyes/monitor** alone (they adapt and deceive) — use scopes and a calibrated display.
- Ignoring **white balance** → color casts that undermine everything.
- Grading for a **look** that fights the story's mood, or with no intentional palette at all.
