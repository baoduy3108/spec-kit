# Mastery Learning

Mastery learning is a simple but powerful idea: **learners advance only after demonstrating they've mastered the current material** — not on a fixed calendar. Time is the variable; **mastery is the constant**. It prevents the accumulation of knowledge gaps that dooms learners in traditional fixed-pace instruction (see adaptive-and-personalized-learning).

## The Problem It Solves

Traditional teaching moves everyone forward on a **fixed schedule** regardless of understanding. A learner who grasped 70% of a unit advances anyway — but that missing 30% becomes a **gap**, and since knowledge is cumulative (later concepts build on earlier ones), gaps **compound**. Soon the learner is lost, not because they're incapable, but because they were pushed forward on shaky foundations. Fixed pace guarantees this for many learners.

## The Mastery Approach

Flip the variables:
1. **Teach a unit**, then use **formative assessment** (see formative-assessment-and-feedback) to check mastery against a clear **threshold** (e.g. 80–90% correct — high enough that the foundation is solid).
2. **If mastered** → advance to the next unit.
3. **If not** → **don't advance**. Instead, provide **targeted remediation** — re-teach differently, more practice, address the specific gaps (see misconception-diagnosis) — then reassess. Loop until mastered.
So every learner builds each concept **solidly** before the next depends on it. Different learners take different amounts of **time**, but all reach mastery — no compounding gaps.

## Why It Works

- **Solid foundations** — later learning stands on mastered prerequisites, so it's easier and gaps don't cascade.
- **Success builds confidence and motivation** (see learner-motivation-and-engagement) — mastery feels good and fuels persistence, vs the demoralization of being perpetually behind.
- It's a key reason one-on-one tutoring is so effective (a tutor won't move on until you get it — see intelligent-tutoring-systems).
It's central to **competency-based education**: progress is defined by demonstrated competencies, not seat time.

## Practical Considerations

- **Set a meaningful mastery threshold** — high enough to ensure a real foundation, but "mastery" ≠ perfection (100% can be an unrealistic gate).
- **Have remediation ready** — mastery learning requires alternative explanations/practice for those who don't pass, not just "try the same thing again."
- **Manage pacing variance** — learners finish units at different times; systems (especially self-paced/adaptive software) handle this well; rigid group settings struggle.
- **Assess mastery validly** — the check must genuinely measure understanding (not memorization or guessing).

## Pitfalls (in understanding/using)

- **Advancing before mastery** (fixed schedule) → compounding gaps and eventual failure — the core problem it fixes.
- **No remediation** — holding a learner back without a *different* approach to help them master it (repeating the same failed teaching).
- **Threshold too low** (weak foundation advances) or **too high/perfectionist** (unrealistic gate, frustration).
- **Invalid mastery checks** — passing on memorization/luck, not real understanding.
- Ignoring **motivation** — endless remediation without encouragement can demoralize (see learner-motivation-and-engagement).
- Trying to run it in a **rigid fixed-pace** group without support for pacing differences.
- Confusing mastery of **facts** with mastery of **application/skill** — assess the right thing.
