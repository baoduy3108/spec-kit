# Time Management & Prioritization

Productivity isn't doing more things — it's doing the **right** things. Most overwhelm comes from treating all tasks as equal.

## Prioritize by Importance, Not Just Urgency

The **Eisenhower matrix** sorts tasks on two axes:
- **Urgent + important** — do now (crises, deadlines).
- **Important, not urgent** — **schedule** it. This is where the highest-value work lives (planning, learning, prevention, relationships) — and it's what urgency constantly crowds out. Protect time for it deliberately.
- **Urgent, not important** — delegate or minimize (many interruptions, some meetings).
- **Neither** — delete.
The trap: living entirely in "urgent," never getting to the important-but-not-urgent work that actually moves things forward.

## Focus on Impact (80/20)

Roughly **20% of tasks produce 80% of the value**. Before working, ask "what's the highest-leverage thing I could do right now?" and do that first. A long to-do list is mostly low-impact — finishing ten trivial items feels productive but isn't. Identify the one or two things that matter and protect them from the busywork.

## Protect Focus

- **Single-task** — multitasking is task-switching, and each switch has a cost (lost context, errors). Do one thing at a time.
- **Timeboxing / focus blocks** — assign a fixed block to a task (or "deep work" sessions with notifications off). A bounded box also fights perfectionism (Parkinson's law: work expands to fill the time given).
- **Batch** similar small tasks (email, messages) into set windows instead of reacting all day.
- **Manage energy, not just time** — do demanding work when your focus is highest.

## Say No & Reduce

Every yes is a no to something else. Guard your important work by declining or deferring low-value requests. A shorter list done well beats a long list half-done.

## Beat Procrastination

Procrastination is usually about the task feeling **too big, unclear, or unpleasant**. Break it into a tiny concrete next step ("open the file and write one sentence"), lower the activation energy, and start — momentum follows action, not motivation. The two-minute rule: if it takes <2 minutes, just do it now.

## Pitfalls

- Confusing **busy with productive** — motion isn't progress.
- Living in **urgent**, never scheduling the **important**.
- **Multitasking** and constant context-switching.
- An **unprioritized** to-do list treated as all-equal.
- Saying yes to everything → no time for what matters.
