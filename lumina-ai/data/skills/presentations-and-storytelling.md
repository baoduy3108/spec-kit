# Presentations & Storytelling

A presentation succeeds when the audience remembers one thing and does what you asked. Most fail by being a data dump read off crowded slides.

## Start From the Audience and the Message

- **Who is the audience** and what do they care about? Tailor the level, framing, and what you emphasize to them, not to what *you* find interesting.
- **What is the ONE message** you want them to remember? If you can't say it in a sentence, the talk isn't ready. Everything serves that message; cut what doesn't.
- **What's the ask** — what do you want them to think, feel, or do at the end?

## Structure as a Story

Facts alone don't persuade or stick; stories do. Use a narrative arc:
- **Setup** — the context/status quo, the world as it is.
- **Tension** — the problem, the conflict, the "but here's what's wrong / what's at stake." This is the hook that makes people care.
- **Resolution** — your solution/insight, and where it leads.
Classic patterns: problem → solution → benefit; or "what is / what could be" tension. A pitch is: problem, why now, solution, why you, the ask. Open with a hook (a question, a surprising stat, a short story), not "agenda."

## Slides: One Idea, Visual

- **One idea per slide** — if a slide has three points, it's three slides.
- **Visuals over text** — the slide supports *you*; it's not your script. Avoid walls of bullets you read aloud (the audience reads faster than you talk and tunes out). A chart, image, or six words beats a paragraph.
- **Big, legible** — readable from the back; high contrast; consistent style.
- The **speaker** carries the content; slides are the backdrop. (For chart design, apply the data-visualization principles.)

## Deliver

- **Open strong, end with the ask** — the first and last 30 seconds are remembered most. End with the clear next step, not "any questions?" trailing off.
- **Signpost** — tell them the structure, then follow it ("three things: … first…").
- **Pace and pause** — slow down; silence after a key point lets it land.
- **Practice out loud and time it** — reading slides ≠ rehearsing; know your opening cold to beat nerves.
- **Anticipate questions**; it's fine to say "I don't know, I'll follow up."

## Pitfalls

- **No single message** — a data dump the audience can't summarize.
- **Reading dense slides** aloud — the fastest way to lose a room.
- **Burying the point** — no hook, no clear ask.
- **Ignoring the audience's** level and interests.
- **Not rehearsing** — clicking through unfamiliar slides live.
