# Crisis Monitoring

Crisis monitoring is detecting and tracking fast-moving, high-stakes real-world events — natural disasters, disease outbreaks, conflicts, unrest, accidents, infrastructure failures — as early and accurately as possible, so responders and decision-makers have **situational awareness**. It combines detection, verification, and live tracking under time pressure.

## Early Warning: Detect It First

The value is in **early** detection — catching a crisis as it emerges, often before official confirmation. Signals:
- **Volume/velocity spikes** — a sudden surge of mentions about an incident in a place (see event-detection-and-alerting) — social media often breaks events before official channels.
- **Keyword/geo triggers** — terms like "earthquake," "explosion," "evacuation" tied to a location.
- **Sensor/official feeds** — seismic, weather, health surveillance, official alerts — authoritative and fast for their domains.
- **Leading indicators** — pre-defined warning signs for anticipated crises (see geopolitical-risk-analysis's indicators & warnings).
Multiple weak signals converging is a strong early signal.

## Verify Under Pressure (the hard tension)

Crises breed **misinformation, panic, and confusion** — early reports are often wrong, exaggerated, or fake (recycled disaster photos are a classic — see information-verification). The tension: you need **speed** *and* **accuracy**. Resolve it by:
- **Corroborating** across independent sources fast (see data-source-reliability) — one alarming post isn't confirmation.
- Weighting **authoritative** sources (official agencies, on-the-ground verified accounts) heavily.
- Marking information **confidence** clearly ("unconfirmed reports of...") rather than presenting rumor as fact.
- Being alert to deliberate disinformation during crises (it spikes exactly then).
Getting it wrong in a crisis causes real harm — but so does being too slow. Communicate uncertainty explicitly.

## Situational Awareness: The Common Operating Picture

The goal is a **live, shared picture** of the evolving situation: what's happening, **where** (map it — see geospatial-mapping-and-geocoding), how severe, who's affected, and how it's changing. Aggregate multi-source data into one coherent view (see real-time-monitoring-dashboards, coordinated-data-views) so everyone works from the same understanding — a "common operating picture." Track scale, spread, and trajectory over time, not just a snapshot.

## Evolve the Assessment

Crises change fast. Monitoring is **continuous re-assessment**: update severity/scope as new information arrives, revise early (possibly wrong) reports, and track the trajectory (escalating? contained? spreading?). Feed it into decision-making with current confidence levels. An assessment from an hour ago may be obsolete.

## Pitfalls (in understanding/using)

- **Amplifying unverified/false** reports in the rush — corroborate; mark confidence; disinformation spikes in crises.
- **Recycled/out-of-context media** treated as current evidence (reverse-search images/video).
- **Too slow** (over-verifying) *or* **too reckless** (spreading rumor) — balance speed and accuracy, convey uncertainty.
- **Single-source** dependence — diversify sources; weight authoritative ones.
- **Snapshot thinking** — not tracking how the crisis evolves; keep re-assessing.
- **No geographic/severity context** — "something happened" isn't actionable; map and scope it.
- Alert fatigue / crying wolf — false crisis alerts erode trust (tune detection — see event-detection-and-alerting).
- Ignoring the **human/ethical** stakes — crisis data involves real people; handle responsibly (privacy, not amplifying panic).
