# Brainstorming Techniques

Good idea generation follows one core rule: **separate generating ideas from judging them.** Mixing the two kills creativity — you censor half-formed ideas before they can grow.

## Diverge, Then Converge

- **Divergent phase** — generate as **many** ideas as possible, wild ones welcome, **no criticism**. Quantity over quality here; you can't evaluate what you haven't generated, and bad ideas spark good ones.
- **Convergent phase** — *afterward*, evaluate, cluster, and select. Apply judgment now, not during generation.
Doing both at once (judging as you go) is the #1 brainstorming failure — people self-censor and the well runs dry.

## The Rules That Make It Work

- **Defer judgment** — no "that won't work" during generation; capture everything.
- **Go for quantity** — aim for a big number; the obvious ideas come first, the interesting ones after you push past them (ideas 5–20 beat 1–4).
- **Welcome wild ideas** — an impractical idea often contains the seed of a practical one; you can tame a wild idea, but you can't wild a tame one.
- **Build on ideas** — "yes, and…" rather than "no, but…"; combine and extend others' contributions.
- **Stay focused** on the question, one conversation at a time.

## Structured Techniques

- **"How Might We…"** — reframe the problem as an open question ("how might we reduce checkout drop-off?") to invite many answers.
- **SCAMPER** — prompt variations on an existing idea: **S**ubstitute, **C**ombine, **A**dapt, **M**odify, **P**ut to other use, **E**liminate, **R**everse. Great for improving/innovating on something.
- **Reverse brainstorming** — ask "how could we make this problem *worse*?" then invert the answers. Surfaces non-obvious fixes and is easier/funnier than direct solving.
- **Brainwriting** — everyone writes ideas silently first, then shares. Beats group verbal brainstorming (which is dominated by loud voices and anchoring) — more, more diverse ideas, and quieter people contribute.
- **Random stimulus** — introduce a random word/image and force connections to the problem, breaking mental ruts.
- **Analogies** — "how does nature/another industry solve this?"

## Facilitating

Set a clear question, timebox the divergent phase, capture everything visibly, enforce "no judgment now," draw out quiet people (brainwriting helps), and only *then* cluster and prioritize. For solo brainstorming, write fast without editing, then review later.

## Pitfalls

- **Judging while generating** — the creativity killer.
- **Stopping too early** — quitting at the first few obvious ideas.
- **Groupthink / anchoring** — everyone converges on the first idea voiced (brainwriting counters this).
- **Loud voices dominating** — quiet good ideas lost.
- **No convergence** — endless ideas, no decision; you must eventually evaluate and choose.
- Vague problem statement → scattered, unusable ideas.
