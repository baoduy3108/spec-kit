# Learning How to Learn

How you study matters more than how long. The most common methods (rereading, highlighting) feel productive but barely work; the effective ones feel harder because they *are* the work.

## Active Recall (the biggest lever)

**Retrieving** information from memory strengthens it far more than reviewing it. So instead of rereading notes, **close the book and test yourself**: "what were the three causes?", explain it aloud, do practice problems. The effort of pulling it out — even struggling — is what builds durable memory. Rereading and highlighting create a false sense of familiarity without recall ability. Turn study material into questions and answer them.

## Spaced Repetition

Review at **increasing intervals** (a day, then three days, then a week…) rather than cramming. Reviewing *just as you're about to forget* is when recall does the most to cement memory. Cramming crams for tomorrow and evaporates; spacing builds long-term retention (this is what flashcard systems like Anki automate). A little, often, beats a lot, once.

## Deliberate Practice

Practice at the **edge of your ability** on the specific weakness, with **immediate feedback**, not comfortable repetition of what you already do well. Mindless repetition plateaus; deliberate practice targets the gap, fails, corrects, repeats. It's uncomfortable by design — that discomfort is the growth.

## More Techniques

- **Interleaving** — mix related topics/problem types in a session rather than blocking one at a time. Harder in the moment, but builds the ability to *choose* the right approach (which real problems require).
- **The Feynman technique** — explain the concept in **simple language as if teaching a beginner**. Where you stumble or reach for jargon reveals what you don't actually understand. Teaching exposes and fills gaps.
- **Understand, don't memorize** — connect new ideas to what you know (build a mental model); understood structure is recalled and transferred; isolated facts aren't.
- **Focused vs diffuse** — intense focus solves problems you understand; stepping away (walk, sleep) lets the diffuse mode connect ideas and crack stuck problems. Both are needed; **sleep consolidates memory** — pulling an all-nighter is counterproductive.

## Practical Loop

Study actively (recall, don't reread) → space the reviews over days → practice deliberately at your weak edge with feedback → explain it simply to find gaps → sleep. Judge learning by what you can *retrieve and apply*, not by time spent or pages reread.

## Pitfalls

- **Passive rereading/highlighting** — feels like studying, builds little.
- **Cramming** — good for tomorrow, gone next week.
- **Comfortable repetition** instead of edge-of-ability practice → plateau.
- **Memorizing without understanding** — brittle, non-transferable.
- Skipping **sleep** — sabotages the consolidation you studied for.
