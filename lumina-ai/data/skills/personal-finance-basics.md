# Personal Finance Basics

General, educational principles for managing money well. (This is not individualized financial advice — specifics depend on your situation, and big decisions may warrant a professional.)

## The Foundation: Spend Less Than You Earn

Every bit of wealth-building starts here. Track where money goes (a simple budget), and keep spending below income so there's a **surplus** to save and invest. A common framework is **50/30/20** — roughly 50% needs, 30% wants, 20% saving/debt — adjust to your reality. **Pay yourself first**: automate saving before you spend, so it happens.

## Emergency Fund First

Before investing, build an **emergency fund** — typically **3–6 months of essential expenses** in an accessible, safe account. This is what stops an unexpected job loss, medical bill, or car repair from forcing you into high-interest debt. It's boring and it's the single most stabilizing financial move.

## Kill High-Interest Debt

**High-interest debt (credit cards, ~20%+) is a financial emergency** — it compounds *against* you faster than almost any investment earns. Prioritize paying it off aggressively (avalanche = highest rate first, mathematically optimal; snowball = smallest balance first, psychologically motivating). Low-interest debt (a cheap mortgage) is less urgent and can coexist with investing.

## Compound Interest (why time matters most)

Money invested grows on its growth — **compounding**. Over decades this is dramatic: starting early beats starting big, because time does the heavy lifting. The same force works *against* you as debt interest. The lesson: **start investing early and consistently**, even small amounts.

## Investing Simply

- **Long-term, diversified, low-cost** — broad index funds spread risk across many companies; low fees keep more of your return (fees compound too). Don't try to pick winners or time the market — most don't beat a simple diversified approach.
- **Match risk to time horizon** — money you need soon stays safe; money for decades away can ride out market ups and downs.
- **Consistency** — invest regularly regardless of market mood (dollar-cost averaging), and leave it alone; panic-selling in downturns locks in losses.
- **Tax-advantaged accounts** (retirement accounts) where available — free efficiency.

## Protect the Downside

**Insurance** (health, and as relevant life/disability/property) protects against catastrophic costs that could wipe out your savings — a key, unglamorous part of a stable plan.

## Priority Order (rough)

1. Budget so you have a surplus → 2. Small starter emergency fund → 3. Pay off high-interest debt → 4. Full emergency fund → 5. Invest consistently for the long term (use tax-advantaged accounts) → 6. Adequate insurance.

## Pitfalls

- **Lifestyle inflation** — spending rises with income, so you never get ahead.
- **No emergency fund** → debt spiral on the first surprise.
- **Carrying high-interest debt** while trying to invest.
- **Trying to time/beat the market**, chasing hot tips, or panic-selling.
- **High fees** quietly eroding returns.
- **No plan / not starting** — the biggest cost is delay, because of compounding.
