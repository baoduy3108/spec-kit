# Positioning Basics

Sharpen positioning before writing a single tagline. Weak positioning is almost always vague audience, weak differentiation, or fuzzy outcomes.

## Answer Five Questions First (all must be specific)

Do not draft a positioning statement until all five have *specific* answers — vague answers are disqualifying; ask a follow-up instead.

1. **WHO** — the specific role/segment (not "businesses") and their current situation.
2. **WHAT** — the concrete problem that triggers action *now*.
3. **HOW** — your actual mechanism, not just a feature list.
4. **WHY** — what you do that competitors *can't or won't*.
5. **SO WHAT** — the measurable transformation/outcome for the customer.

## Statement Template

> "We are the only **[category]** for **[specific customer]** who **[specific situation]**. Unlike **[named alternative]**, we **[key differentiator]**."

Use **real competitor names** — no placeholders.

## Quality Gates (must pass all five)

- **Specificity** — a stranger can tell exactly who it's for.
- **Differentiation** — the "unlike" clause is real, not a generic claim everyone makes ("high quality", "great support").
- **Credibility** — you can back the claim with evidence.
- **Meaningfulness** — the outcome matters to the customer.
- **Memorability** — it survives being repeated once.

## Self-Critique After Drafting

Did you name actual companies (not "other tools")? Did you test clarity with a non-expert? Are the differentiators specific rather than table-stakes? Does the positioning match existing customer evidence (their words, their reasons for buying)?

## Diagnosing Weak Positioning

If it feels weak, isolate the cause: **audience** too broad (tighten WHO), **differentiation** generic (rebuild WHY from first principles), or **outcome** vague (make SO WHAT measurable). Fix the specific gap rather than rewording the whole thing.
