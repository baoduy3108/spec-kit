# Trading Psychology

Most trading failure isn't from bad strategies — it's from **not following them** under emotional pressure. Markets are an arena where fear, greed, and cognitive biases directly cost money, and the discipline to act consistently against your instincts is often the deciding skill. *(Educational — not financial advice; applies to any high-stakes decision-making.)*

## Why Emotions Sabotage Trading

Trading triggers powerful emotions because real money and uncertainty are involved, and our instincts are **exactly wrong** for markets. The core emotional forces:
- **Fear** — cutting winners too early (to lock in a small gain), hesitating on good setups, panic-selling at bottoms.
- **Greed** — oversizing, chasing, refusing to take profits, ignoring risk when things go well.
These push you to buy high (greed/FOMO) and sell low (fear/panic) — the opposite of what works.

## The Key Biases

- **Loss aversion** — losses hurt about twice as much as equivalent gains feel good (see decision-making-frameworks). This makes traders **hold losers too long** (hoping to avoid realizing the loss — "it'll come back") and **sell winners too soon** (to secure a gain) — precisely backwards from "cut losses, let winners run."
- **Revenge trading** — after a loss, trading impulsively to "win it back," abandoning rules — usually compounding the loss. One of the fastest ways to blow up.
- **FOMO (fear of missing out)** — chasing a move that's already run, entering late at bad prices, oversizing to catch up.
- **Overconfidence** — after wins, attributing luck to skill, sizing up, and abandoning discipline right before a reversal (see critical-thinking).
- **Confirmation bias** — seeing only what supports your position, ignoring warning signs.
- **Sunk-cost fallacy** — averaging down into a losing position because you're already committed.

## Building Discipline

The antidote is **process over outcome** and **rules over impulse**:
- **A written plan** — predefine entries, exits, position sizes, and risk limits (see risk-management-in-trading) **before** trading, when you're calm — then **follow it** mechanically, removing in-the-moment emotional decisions.
- **Focus on process, not P&L** — judge yourself on **following your rules**, not on whether a single trade won (a good decision can lose; a bad one can win — see decision-making-frameworks). Over many trades, good process wins.
- **Accept losses as a cost of business** — losing trades are inevitable even with an edge; a single loss means nothing. Pre-accepting this defuses the fear/revenge cycle.
- **Journaling** — record every trade and your reasoning/emotions; review to spot recurring mistakes and patterns (see note-taking-systems). Self-awareness is the foundation of improvement.
- **Manage yourself** — trade sizes small enough that emotion doesn't take over; step away after big wins/losses; don't trade tilted.

## Pitfalls (in understanding/using)

- **Holding losers, cutting winners** (loss aversion) — the exact opposite of what works; honor stops, let winners run.
- **Revenge trading** after a loss → abandoning rules, compounding losses.
- **FOMO / chasing** — entering late at bad prices, oversizing to catch up.
- **Overconfidence** after a winning streak → sizing up and dropping discipline before a reversal.
- Judging yourself on **individual outcomes** rather than **process** (luck vs skill confusion).
- Trading **too big** — position sizes where emotion overrides your plan.
- No **plan/journal** — trading on impulse, never learning from mistakes.
- Believing you're immune to these — everyone feels them; discipline (systems/rules) beats willpower.
