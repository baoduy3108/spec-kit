# Note-Taking Systems

Notes are worthless if you never find or reuse them. Good note-taking is about **thinking and future retrieval**, not capturing everything verbatim.

## Capture in Your Own Words

The biggest mistake is transcribing verbatim (copy-paste, word-for-word). **Rephrasing in your own words** forces you to *understand* it (the same principle as the Feynman technique — see learning-how-to-learn), and produces notes future-you can actually use. A highlight you didn't process is a note you'll never revisit. Capture the **idea and why it matters to you**, not the raw text.

## Capture Fast, Organize Later

Lower the friction to capture (a quick inbox/daily note) so ideas don't get lost, then process/organize in a separate pass. Trying to file perfectly at capture time creates friction that stops you capturing at all. **Capture → clarify → organize → review.**

## Organize for Retrieval

The value is in getting the note back when relevant. Two popular approaches:
- **PARA** (Projects, Areas, Resources, Archives) — organize by **actionability**: what you're working on now vs ongoing responsibilities vs reference vs done. Action-oriented.
- **Zettelkasten** ("slip-box") — many small **atomic notes** (one idea each) that **link** to related notes, building a web of connected thought. Over time the links surface unexpected connections and become a genuine thinking tool. Tags and links beat rigid folders for ideas that belong in many places.
Use folders/tags/links so a note is findable by how you'll *look for* it later, not by where it happened to come from. Search matters — name and phrase notes so you can find them.

## Progressive Summarization & Review

- **Progressive summarization** — on revisits, distill: highlight the key lines, then bold the essential, so a note becomes scannable at a glance without losing detail. You refine notes as they prove useful.
- **Review** — notes you never revisit don't help. Periodically revisit (weekly review, resurfacing) to reinforce and connect. A note system with no review is a write-only graveyard.

## Match the Tool to the Job

- **Fleeting/quick** capture — a fast inbox (phone note, daily note).
- **Reference/knowledge** — a linked note app (Obsidian, Notion, etc.).
- **Tasks** — a task manager, *not* buried in prose notes (see time-management-and-prioritization).
Don't over-engineer the system — the best system is the one you actually use consistently; elaborate setups often become procrastination.

## Why It Matters

Notes are a "second brain" — offloading memory so you can think with more than fits in your head, and connecting ideas over time. But only if they're processed (your words), organized for retrieval, and reviewed. Otherwise they're digital hoarding.

## Pitfalls / Notes

- **Verbatim capture** without processing → unusable, un-understood notes.
- **Collector's fallacy** — saving/highlighting feels productive but isn't learning; you must engage with it.
- **Over-engineering** the system instead of using it.
- **No review** → write-only pile you never benefit from.
- **Rigid folders** for ideas that belong in many places → use links/tags.
- Mixing tasks into reference notes → things to *do* get lost.
