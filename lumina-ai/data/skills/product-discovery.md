# Product Discovery

Product discovery is the work of figuring out **what's worth building** — before you spend months building it. Most product failures aren't bad execution; they're **building something nobody wanted**. Discovery de-risks that by understanding real problems first.

## Problems Before Solutions

The cardinal rule: **fall in love with the problem, not your solution.** Customers (and founders) jump to solutions ("build me an X"), but the solution is often wrong even when the underlying problem is real. Dig for the **problem**: what are they trying to accomplish, what's painful today, what happens if it's not solved? A well-understood problem admits many solutions; a solution chasing no real problem is dead on arrival.

## Jobs To Be Done (a powerful lens)

People "hire" products to make progress in their lives — the **job** they're trying to get done. "I don't want a drill, I want a hole" — and really, a shelf, and really, a tidy home. Understanding the underlying job (functional + emotional + social) reveals the true competition and the real need, not just feature requests. Ask what job your product is hired for, and what it's competing against (often "doing nothing" or a spreadsheet).

## Customer Interviews (done right)

Talk to real users — but **interviews are easy to do badly**:
- **Ask about the past, not the future** — "tell me about the last time you faced this" beats "would you use X?" (people are terrible at predicting their own behavior and will politely lie to be nice).
- **Dig into specifics and emotions** — real stories, real workarounds, real frustration reveal genuine pain.
- **Don't pitch or lead** — you're learning, not selling. Leading questions get you the flattering answers that kill startups (see *The Mom Test*).
- **Listen for what they already do** — existing workarounds/hacks signal a real, urgent problem.

## Validate Demand Cheaply

Before building, test whether people actually want it, at low cost:
- **Signals of real need** — are they currently paying, hacking a workaround, or asking repeatedly?
- **Lightweight tests** — landing pages, mockups, concierge/manual MVPs, pre-orders, fake-door tests (see mvp-and-validation).
- Distinguish **"that's interesting"** (polite) from **"take my money / when can I have it"** (real).

## Pitfalls (in understanding/using)

- **Building first, validating never** — the #1 way to waste months on unwanted features.
- **Leading/pitching in interviews** → you hear what you want, not the truth (use The Mom Test approach).
- Asking about the **hypothetical future** ("would you...") instead of past behavior.
- Confusing **feature requests** with underlying needs — solve the job, not the literal ask.
- Talking to too few / the wrong people, or only friendly ones who won't be honest.
- Falling in love with your **solution** and ignoring disconfirming evidence.
- Treating discovery as a one-time phase — it's continuous (see mvp-and-validation, user-research).
