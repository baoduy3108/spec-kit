# B2B Sales Fundamentals

B2B (business-to-business) selling is a **process** of helping a company decide to buy — usually with a larger deal size, longer cycle, and multiple decision-makers than consumer sales. Understanding the fundamentals helps founders, engineers, and PMs sell (and understand their buyers).

## The Pipeline

Sales is a pipeline of stages, each with a conversion rate:
1. **Prospecting** — find potential customers (fitting your ICP — see go-to-market-strategy).
2. **Qualifying** — determine if they're a real, worthwhile opportunity (below).
3. **Discovery** — deeply understand their problem, situation, and needs.
4. **Proposal/demo** — show how you solve their specific problem.
5. **Negotiation/close** — agree terms, handle objections, sign.
6. **Onboard/expand** — deliver value; land-and-expand.
Managing the pipeline (knowing where each deal is, conversion rates, where deals stall) is core sales discipline.

## Qualify Early (don't waste time)

Not every lead is worth pursuing. Qualification frameworks force the key questions:
- **BANT** — Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline.
- **MEDDIC** — Metrics, Economic buyer, Decision criteria, Decision process, Identify pain, Champion.
Qualify **hard and early** — disqualifying a bad-fit deal is a win (saves months). Chasing unqualified deals is the biggest time sink in sales.

## Discovery & Consultative Selling

The best selling is **consultative**: ask questions, understand the customer's real problem and its cost, and position your product as the solution to **their** specific pain — not a feature dump. Listen more than you talk. This mirrors product discovery (see product-discovery) — you're diagnosing before prescribing. Quantify the pain (what's it costing them?) to justify the price (see pricing-strategy).

## Multiple Stakeholders & Champions

B2B deals involve several people: the **economic buyer** (controls budget), **users**, **influencers**, and often a **blocker**. Find and empower a **champion** — an internal advocate who wants your solution and will sell it inside their org when you're not in the room. Map the stakeholders and their concerns; a deal dies if any key player isn't addressed.

## Objections & Follow-Up

- **Objections are information** — surface and address concerns (price, risk, timing, competition) directly rather than avoiding them; often they signal genuine interest.
- **Follow up persistently and helpfully** — most deals need multiple touches; deals die from silence, not rejection. Add value each touch (a relevant resource, an answer), don't just "check in."

## Pitfalls (in understanding/using)

- **Not qualifying** — chasing bad-fit deals wastes months; disqualify early.
- **Pitching/feature-dumping** instead of discovering the customer's real pain and selling to it.
- Ignoring the **buying committee** — selling to one person while other stakeholders block it.
- No **champion** — no one advocates internally and the deal stalls.
- **Weak follow-up** — letting deals go cold; persistence (with value) wins.
- Discounting to close instead of **justifying value** (trains buyers, erodes margin — see pricing-strategy).
- Talking more than listening — you learn (and sell) by understanding their problem.
