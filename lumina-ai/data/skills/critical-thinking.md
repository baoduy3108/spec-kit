# Critical Thinking

Critical thinking is evaluating claims and reasoning honestly — including your own — rather than accepting what's persuasive, familiar, or what you already believe.

## Evaluate the Claim and the Evidence

- **What's the actual claim, and what would prove/disprove it?** Vague claims that can't be tested aren't strong.
- **Consider the source** — expertise, incentives, and bias. Not a reason to dismiss (that's ad hominem), but a reason to scrutinize. Follow the incentives.
- **Quality of evidence** — anecdote vs data, sample size, is it representative, is it cherry-picked? One vivid story isn't a trend.
- **Correlation ≠ causation** — the biggest trap. Two things moving together may share a hidden cause, be coincidence, or run the other direction (see statistics-fundamentals). Ask "what else could explain this?"
- **Base rates** — a scary-sounding number is meaningless without the base rate (see probability-and-bayes).

## Logical Fallacies (spot the cheat)

- **Ad hominem** — attacking the person, not the argument.
- **Straw man** — refuting a distorted, weaker version of the opponent's claim.
- **False dilemma** — "either A or B" when other options exist.
- **Appeal to authority/popularity** — "an expert/everyone says so" isn't proof (evidence is).
- **Slippery slope, circular reasoning, hasty generalization, whataboutism, appeal to emotion.**
A persuasive argument can be built entirely of these — persuasiveness isn't validity.

## Cognitive Biases (your own mind cheating)

- **Confirmation bias** — noticing evidence that fits your view, ignoring what doesn't. The default failure mode; counter it by **actively seeking disconfirming evidence**.
- **Anchoring** — over-weighting the first number/idea.
- **Availability** — judging likelihood by how easily examples come to mind (vivid ≠ common).
- **Motivated reasoning** — reaching the conclusion you *want*, then justifying it.
The hardest bias to see is your own — assume you have them.

## Habits of a Critical Thinker

- **Steelman the opposing view** — state the *strongest* version of what you disagree with before rejecting it. If you can't, you don't understand it.
- **Ask "how would I know if I'm wrong?"** — seek falsification, not just support.
- **Separate what you know from what you assume**; hold conclusions proportional to the evidence, and update when evidence changes.
- **Consider alternative explanations** before settling on one.
- **Follow the evidence, not the desired conclusion.**

## Pitfalls

- **Confirmation bias** — the pervasive one.
- **Confusing persuasive with true** — rhetoric and fallacies feel convincing.
- **Correlation as causation.**
- **Ignoring base rates** and sample quality.
- **Certainty beyond the evidence** — and refusing to update.
