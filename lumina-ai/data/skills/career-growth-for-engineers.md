# Career Growth for Engineers

Advancing as a software engineer is less about writing more code and more about **increasing your impact and leverage**. Understanding what actually drives growth (and what your organization rewards) helps you grow deliberately instead of hoping seniority arrives on its own.

## From Output to Impact

Junior engineers are measured by **output** (tasks completed, code written). Senior+ engineers are measured by **impact** — the value and outcomes they drive, often *through others*. The mindset shift: it's not "how much did I code" but "how much did I move the needle for the team/product/business." At higher levels, **making others more effective** (unblocking, mentoring, good design decisions) counts more than your personal commits. Optimize for impact, not activity.

## Levels & Scope

Engineering **ladders** (junior → mid → senior → staff → principal) are largely about growing **scope** and **autonomy**:
- Junior: complete well-defined tasks with guidance.
- Mid: own features independently.
- Senior: own ambiguous problems/projects end-to-end, make sound technical decisions, mentor.
- Staff+: impact across teams/org — technical strategy, hard cross-cutting problems, multiplying others.
Growth = handling **larger, more ambiguous scope** with **less oversight** and **more influence**. To get promoted, operate at the next level *before* the title (demonstrate the scope), and make sure the right people see it.

## The IC vs Management Fork

At senior+, a fork appears:
- **Individual Contributor (IC)** track — deepen technical impact (staff/principal engineer). You don't have to become a manager to grow; strong IC ladders exist.
- **Management** track — grow impact through leading people.
They're **different jobs**, not a hierarchy (a staff engineer ≈ a manager in level). Choose based on what energizes you (deep technical work vs enabling people), not on a belief that management is "up." You can switch.

## What Actually Drives Growth

- **Ownership** — take responsibility for outcomes, not just assigned tasks; own problems end-to-end.
- **Judgment** — increasingly you're paid for good decisions under ambiguity (trade-offs, what to build, what not to — see decision-making-frameworks, anti-over-engineering).
- **Communication** — writing, explaining, aligning; senior work is deeply about communication (see communicating-concisely, technical-writing). Great engineers who can't communicate stall.
- **Visibility** — do impactful work *and* make it visible (not bragging — keeping stakeholders informed, writing up wins, presenting). Invisible impact is under-rewarded.
- **Leverage** — mentoring, tooling, documentation, and design decisions that make *many* people more productive.

## Owning Your Growth

- **Seek feedback** actively and act on it (see giving-and-receiving-feedback).
- **Find mentors/sponsors** — mentors advise; sponsors advocate for you in rooms you're not in.
- **Have growth conversations** with your manager — know the ladder's expectations and where you stand; make your ambitions known.
- **Keep learning** deliberately (see learning-how-to-learn) — the field moves.

## Pitfalls (in understanding/using)

- Equating growth with **more code/hours** instead of **impact and leverage**.
- Assuming promotion comes automatically from tenure — you must demonstrate next-level scope and make it visible.
- Believing **management is the only way up** — strong IC (staff/principal) tracks exist; pick what fits you.
- **Invisible work** — high impact nobody knows about; communicate it (informatively, not boastfully).
- Neglecting **communication/soft skills** — they gate senior growth as much as technical skill.
- Staying in a comfortable **narrow scope** — growth requires taking on ambiguity and ownership.
- Not seeking **feedback/sponsorship** or having explicit career conversations with your manager.
