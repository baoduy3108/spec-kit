# Storyboarding & Shot Planning

A storyboard is a **shot-by-shot visual plan** of a video — sketches or frames showing what each shot looks like, in sequence, before you produce it. It's how filmmakers (and AI-video creators) think through the visuals, sequence, and story flow **before** committing to expensive production or generation (see camera-and-cinematography-basics, generative-media-pipeline).

## Why Plan Before Producing

Production (or AI generation) is **costly and slow** (see how-video-generation-works). Discovering that shots don't cut together, the story doesn't flow, or a scene doesn't work **after** producing it wastes enormous effort. Storyboarding is cheap thinking done up front: sketch the shots, arrange the sequence, and catch problems on paper. It's the visual equivalent of an outline (see planning-and-task-breakdown) — plan the expensive work before doing it.

## What a Storyboard Captures

Each panel/frame plans a **shot**:
- **Framing** — shot size and composition (see camera-and-cinematography-basics): what's in frame, from what angle.
- **Action** — what happens in the shot, subject positions and movement.
- **Camera movement** — pans, dollies, zooms (arrows indicate motion).
- **Sequence & timing** — the order of shots and roughly how long each lasts.
- **Notes** — dialogue, sound, transitions, intent.
Together the panels tell the whole video visually, revealing its **rhythm and flow**.

## Sequencing & Flow

The power of a storyboard is seeing how shots **cut together** into a coherent sequence:
- **Establish then focus** — often a wide/establishing shot for context, then closer shots for detail/emotion (see camera-and-cinematography-basics).
- **Vary shots** — mix sizes/angles to maintain interest and rhythm; avoid monotony.
- **Visual flow** — each shot should lead naturally to the next; plan transitions and pacing.
- **Match the story beats** — the shot sequence should serve the narrative arc (see presentations-and-storytelling, motion-and-storytelling).

## Continuity

Storyboarding helps maintain **continuity** — consistency across shots so the audience isn't jarred: consistent screen direction (the 180-degree rule — subjects keep the same relative positions/eyelines across cuts), consistent lighting/props/appearance, and logical spatial relationships. Planning shots together catches continuity errors before they happen. (This is a **major challenge for AI video** — keeping a character/scene consistent across separately-generated shots — so planning matters even more.)

## For AI-Generated Video

Storyboarding is especially valuable for AI video: plan each shot's content, framing, and the exact **prompt/reference** (see prompt-engineering-for-visual-media), decide keyframes (see keyframe-animation-and-interpolation), and plan for the **consistency challenge** — reuse reference images/characters across shots, and design the sequence knowing each clip is short. The storyboard becomes the shot list you generate against and assemble (see generative-media-pipeline).

## Pitfalls (in understanding/using)

- **Producing/generating without planning** → shots that don't cut together, wasted effort, no coherent story.
- **Monotonous shot choices** — same size/angle throughout; vary them for rhythm.
- **Ignoring continuity** — jarring cuts, inconsistent direction/eyelines (180-degree rule), props/lighting jumps.
- **No sense of flow/pacing** — shots that don't lead into each other or match story beats.
- For AI video: not planning for **cross-shot consistency** (characters/scenes drift) — reuse references, plan around short clips.
- Over-detailing the storyboard when a rough plan suffices (it's a thinking tool, not final art).
- Forgetting **timing/duration** — a storyboard is about sequence *and* rhythm.
