# Pricing Strategy

Pricing is one of the highest-leverage decisions in a business — a small price change flows straight to profit, and it signals your product's value. Yet it's often set by gut. The core shift: price on **value to the customer**, not your costs.

## Three Ways to Set Price

- **Cost-plus** — add a margin to your costs. Simple, but ignores what customers will actually pay; leaves money on the table or overprices.
- **Competitive** — price relative to competitors. A reference point, but a race to the bottom if you compete only on price and ignore differentiation (see positioning-basics).
- **Value-based** (usually best) — price on the **value/outcome the customer gets** and their **willingness to pay**. If you save a business $100k, $20k is a bargain regardless of your costs. This captures the most value and forces you to understand and communicate your value.

## Willingness to Pay

The real question isn't "what does it cost us" but "**what is it worth to them**." Research it: interviews, surveys (van Westendorp), experiments, and segmentation (different customers value it differently). Price to the value you deliver to each segment. Willingness to pay is also a **product-market-fit signal** — if no one will pay, reconsider the value.

## Tiering & Anchoring

- **Good/Better/Best** tiers let customers self-select by needs and willingness to pay, expanding your market and increasing average revenue. Most pick the middle.
- **Anchoring** — a high-priced tier makes the middle look reasonable (the expensive option sells the mid option). The first price a customer sees anchors their perception.
- **Align the metric** to value — charge on the dimension that scales with the value customers get (seats, usage, transactions) so price grows with their success (see saas-metrics).

## Models

- **Subscription** — predictable recurring revenue (see saas-metrics).
- **Usage-based** — pay for what you use; scales with value, low entry barrier, but less predictable.
- **Freemium** — free tier for adoption, paid for power features; works when the free tier drives conversion/virality without cannibalizing paid.
- **Per-seat, tiered, hybrid** — combine to fit how customers derive value.

## Psychological Factors

Charm pricing ($9.99), price framing (per-day vs per-year), decoy options, and bundling all affect perception. Small levers, but real. Test them.

## Pitfalls (in understanding/using)

- **Underpricing** — the most common mistake; founders anchor on their costs/fears, not customer value, and leave huge money (and perceived value) on the table.
- **Cost-plus** when value-based would capture far more.
- **Competing on price** without differentiation → margin erosion and a signal of low value.
- Charging on a **metric misaligned** with value (customer's cost grows without their benefit).
- **Never testing/raising** prices — pricing isn't set-and-forget; revisit as value and market change.
- Too **many tiers/options** → choice paralysis; keep it simple (3-ish).
- Racing to the bottom with **discounts** that train customers to wait and devalue the product.
