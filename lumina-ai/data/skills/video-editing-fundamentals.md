# Video Editing Fundamentals

Editing is where footage becomes a **story** — the craft of choosing which shots to use, in what order, and for how long. It's often called the "invisible art": good editing you don't notice; it just flows. Understanding it is essential for assembling any video, including stitching AI-generated clips (see generative-media-pipeline).

## The Cut Is Everything

The fundamental unit of editing is the **cut** — the instant transition from one shot to another. Most editing is cuts (fancy transitions are the exception). The editor's core decisions:
- **Which shots** to use (and cut out).
- **What order** to place them.
- **When to cut** — the timing of each cut, which sets pacing and rhythm.
- **What to cut to** — the next shot that best serves the story/emotion.
Every cut is a choice that shapes meaning and feel.

## Pacing & Rhythm

The **rhythm** of cuts drives energy and emotion:
- **Fast cutting** (short shots) → energy, tension, excitement (action, montages).
- **Slow cutting** (long shots) → calm, contemplation, tension of a different kind (drama, atmosphere).
- **Vary the rhythm** to match story beats; monotonous pacing (all fast or all slow) tires the viewer.
Pacing is one of the most powerful tools an editor has — the same footage cut fast vs slow feels completely different.

## Continuity vs Montage

Two editing philosophies:
- **Continuity editing** — cuts that preserve **spatial/temporal continuity** so the sequence feels seamless and real (match on action, consistent screen direction — see storyboarding-and-shot-planning). The invisible, standard style.
- **Montage** — juxtaposing shots to create meaning, compress time, or evoke emotion (a training montage, a passage-of-time sequence). Cuts that create ideas by association, not continuity.

## Techniques

- **Cutting on action** — cut in the middle of a movement (a person sitting, turning); the motion carries across the cut and hides it (smoother than cutting on stillness).
- **J-cuts and L-cuts** — the **audio** of the next/previous shot leads or lags the picture cut. This is a secret of professional editing: **sound doesn't cut at the same time as picture**, which makes transitions feel natural and connected (audio bridges the cut).
- **Match cuts** — cut between visually/thematically similar frames (a smooth or meaningful transition).
- **Transitions** — cuts (default), dissolves (time passing, softer), fades (scene/section boundaries). Use fancy transitions **sparingly** — overusing wipes/effects looks amateur; the cut is usually best.
- **Audio is half the edit** — music, sound effects, and rhythm shape emotion and pacing enormously (see audio-and-music-generation). Edit to the sound, not just the picture.

## Assembling a Story

Editing turns clips into narrative: establish, develop, and pay off; maintain flow (see presentations-and-storytelling); cut out anything that doesn't serve the story ("kill your darlings"). The goal is a coherent, flowing whole — not just a sequence of nice shots.

## Pitfalls (in understanding/using)

- **Overusing flashy transitions** (wipes, spins, effects) → amateur; the **cut** is usually best; use dissolves/fades purposefully.
- **Monotonous pacing** — all cuts the same length → no rhythm; vary to the story.
- **Cutting on stillness** instead of **on action** → more visible, jarring cuts.
- **Ignoring audio** — cutting picture and sound at the exact same time (stiff); use J/L-cuts, and remember audio drives emotion.
- **Keeping too much** — not cutting footage that doesn't serve the story (indulgent, slow).
- Breaking **continuity** (screen direction, eyelines, match on action) → disorienting cuts.
- For AI clips: expecting to stitch short generated clips without attention to **consistency and rhythm** (see generative-media-pipeline).
