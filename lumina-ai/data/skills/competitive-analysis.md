# Competitive Analysis

Competitive analysis is understanding the alternatives your customers could choose, to find where you can **win** and how to **position** (see positioning-basics). Done well, it sharpens your strategy; done poorly, it turns into blindly copying competitors' features.

## Identify the Real Competition

Competitors aren't just the obvious rivals:
- **Direct** — solve the same problem the same way (another app in your category).
- **Indirect** — solve the same problem differently (a spreadsheet vs your SaaS).
- **Substitutes / "do nothing"** — the customer's status quo, a manual workaround, or simply not solving it. **This is often your biggest competitor** — inertia. If you only benchmark against direct rivals, you miss why customers actually don't switch.
Frame competition around the **job to be done** (see product-discovery), not just your product category.

## Compare on What Customers Care About

Build a comparison across the **dimensions that matter to customers** (not a giant feature checklist): the outcomes, the key jobs, price, ease, trust, support, integrations. A **competitive matrix** (you vs alternatives across these axes) reveals where you're strong, weak, and — crucially — where there's an **unoccupied gap** you can own. Avoid vanity comparisons where you cherry-pick axes you happen to win.

## Understand Their Strategy, Not Just Features

Look beyond features to **why** they make their choices: their target segment, positioning, pricing model, business model, and strengths/weaknesses. Read their marketing (who they target, what they emphasize), pricing pages, reviews (what customers praise/complain about — gold for finding gaps), and public strategy. Understanding their strategy tells you where they **can't** or **won't** follow you — the basis of durable differentiation.

## Find Your Differentiation

The goal isn't to be better on every axis (impossible and unfocused) — it's to be **distinctly better on the axes your ICP values most**, ideally where competitors are weak or structurally constrained. Pick a position you can own and defend, and lean into it. Differentiation > imitation.

## Pitfalls (in understanding/using)

- **Feature-copying** — chasing every competitor feature turns you into a worse copy of them and abandons your own strategy. Differentiate, don't mirror.
- Ignoring **"do nothing"/status quo** — often the real competitor; understand the switching inertia.
- Defining competitors too **narrowly** (only direct rivals) → blindsided by substitutes/indirect.
- **Cherry-picking** flattering comparison axes — analyze on what customers value, honestly.
- Analyzing once and never updating — competition and markets move.
- Being **reactive** (following competitors' roadmaps) instead of strategic (owning your position).
- Underestimating incumbents' distribution/brand advantages or overestimating a feature edge.
