# Copywriting Basics

Copywriting is writing that gets the reader to act. It's not about clever words — it's about the *reader's* desires and a clear path to "yes."

## Benefits, Not Features

The #1 rule: sell the **benefit** (what the reader gets), not the **feature** (what the product has). "1GB storage" is a feature; "1,000 songs in your pocket" is the benefit. People buy outcomes and transformations, not specs. For every feature, ask "so what?" until you reach the human payoff, and lead with that.

## Know the Reader and Their Pain

Great copy starts from the **audience**: who they are, what they want, what problem keeps them up at night, and the words *they* use. Speak to their specific pain and desired outcome. Generic copy ("we provide quality solutions") persuades no one; copy that names the reader's exact frustration and the relief they want does.

## Clear Over Clever

**Clarity beats cleverness.** A confused reader doesn't buy. Simple, concrete, specific language ("cancel anytime, no fees") beats vague hype ("revolutionary synergy"). Cut jargon and filler; short sentences; specific numbers and proof over adjectives.

## Proven Structures

- **AIDA** — **Attention** (a hook/headline that stops them), **Interest** (relevant benefit), **Desire** (make them want it, with proof), **Action** (tell them exactly what to do).
- **PAS** — **Problem** (name their pain), **Agitate** (make it vivid, the cost of not solving it), **Solution** (your offer as the relief). Very effective because it starts from the reader's felt problem.

## The Essentials

- **Headline** — most people read only the headline; it does 80% of the work. Make it about the reader's benefit/outcome or curiosity (see homepage-audit).
- **One clear call to action** — tell them the single next step, specifically ("Start your free trial"), and don't dilute it with competing asks.
- **Proof/credibility** — testimonials, numbers, guarantees, specifics reduce the risk of saying yes (see marketing-principles).
- **Voice** — match the brand and audience; write like a human talking to one person, not a corporation broadcasting.

## Process

Research the reader → pick the one core benefit → hook them (headline) → build desire with benefits + proof → remove risk → one clear CTA. Then **cut ruthlessly** — the second draft, half the length, is almost always stronger.

## Pitfalls

- **Feature-dumping** instead of benefits.
- **Talking about yourself** ("we", "our") instead of the reader ("you", their outcome).
- **Clever/vague** copy that sacrifices clarity.
- **Weak or multiple CTAs** — the reader doesn't know what to do.
- **No proof** — claims without credibility.
- Writing for everyone (so it lands with no one) — write to a specific person.
