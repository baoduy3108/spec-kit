# Bloom's Taxonomy & Learning Objectives

Bloom's taxonomy is a framework for classifying **levels of cognitive complexity** in learning, and it's the backbone of writing clear **learning objectives** — statements of what a learner should be able to do. Together they make teaching intentional: you know what level of thinking you're targeting and can align teaching and assessment to it.

## The Hierarchy of Cognitive Levels

Bloom's (revised) taxonomy orders thinking from lower to higher order:
1. **Remember** — recall facts (define, list, name).
2. **Understand** — explain ideas in your own words (describe, summarize, explain).
3. **Apply** — use knowledge in new situations (solve, use, demonstrate).
4. **Analyze** — break down and see relationships (compare, differentiate, examine).
5. **Evaluate** — judge based on criteria (critique, justify, assess).
6. **Create** — produce something new (design, build, compose).
Higher levels generally **build on** lower ones (you can't analyze what you don't understand), but real learning isn't strictly linear — the point is recognizing that "knowing facts" (remember) is very different from "using and creating" (apply/create), and much teaching stops too low.

## Why It Matters: Target Higher-Order Thinking

A lot of instruction and assessment lingers at **remember/understand** (memorize and regurgitate) when the goal is really **apply/analyze/create** (use the knowledge). Bloom's makes the target **explicit**: decide what cognitive level you actually want, and design for it. Deep learning and transfer come from the higher levels — but they require the lower foundations, so sequence appropriately (see mastery-learning, scaffolding-and-zpd).

## Writing Learning Objectives

A good objective states **what the learner will be able to do**, observably and measurably: *"By the end, the learner can [action verb] [content] [context/criteria]."* Keys:
- **Measurable action verbs** — use verbs you can **observe/assess** ("solve," "compare," "design"), **not** vague ones ("understand," "know," "appreciate" — you can't directly measure "understand"). The verb encodes the Bloom's level.
- **Learner-centered** — what the *learner* does, not what the teacher covers.
- **Specific and assessable** — clear enough that you can tell whether it was achieved.
Objectives guide everything: they tell you what to teach, what to assess, and the level to aim for.

## Alignment (the crucial payoff)

The power of objectives is **constructive alignment**: teaching, activities, and **assessment** should all match the objective's level. If your objective is "apply" but your test only asks learners to "recall," you're assessing the wrong thing — and learners will study for recall, not application. Align the cognitive level across objective → instruction → assessment. Mismatch is a common, quiet failure.

## Pitfalls (in understanding/using)

- **Objectives with unmeasurable verbs** ("understand," "know") — can't assess or design for them; use observable action verbs.
- **Teaching/testing at a lower level than intended** — objective says "analyze," assessment asks "recall" (misalignment).
- **Staying at remember/understand** when the goal is apply/create — memorization mistaken for learning.
- **Teacher-centered** objectives ("cover topic X") instead of learner-centered ("learner can do X").
- Treating the hierarchy as **strictly linear/rigid** — it's a guide to complexity, not a rulebook.
- Writing objectives and then **not aligning** teaching and assessment to them (the whole point).
- Vague or too many objectives — keep them focused and assessable.
