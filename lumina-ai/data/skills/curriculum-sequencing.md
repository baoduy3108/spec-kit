# Curriculum Sequencing

Curriculum sequencing is deciding **what to teach in what order** — one of the most consequential design choices in education. A well-sequenced curriculum builds understanding step by step; a badly-sequenced one leaves learners lost because later topics depend on foundations they don't have. It's the map that adaptive learning paths navigate (see adaptive-and-personalized-learning, mastery-learning).

## Respect Prerequisites (the foundation)

Knowledge is **cumulative** — most concepts depend on earlier ones. The primary rule: **teach prerequisites before what needs them.** You can't teach multiplication before addition, or recursion before functions. Map the **dependency graph** of concepts (what must be understood before what — a DAG, see topological-sort) and sequence so every topic is reached only after its prerequisites are solid. Violating this is the top cause of "I'm lost" — the learner lacks a foundation the current topic assumes.

## Ordering Principles

Beyond prerequisites, several principles guide order:
- **Simple → complex** — start with basic cases, add complexity gradually (manages cognitive load — see cognitive-load-theory).
- **Concrete → abstract** — begin with tangible examples/experiences, then generalize to abstract principles (learners grasp concrete first, then build the abstraction).
- **Known → unknown** — connect new material to what learners already know (build on existing schemas).
- **Whole → parts → whole** — sometimes give the big picture first (why this matters, where it's going) so details have context, then dive into parts.

## Spiral Curriculum

Rather than teaching a topic once and moving on forever, a **spiral curriculum** **revisits** key concepts multiple times, each at **greater depth** and sophistication. First exposure builds basic familiarity; later passes deepen and connect it. This leverages spacing (see spaced-repetition-systems) and lets understanding mature — you don't have to fully master everything on first contact. Big ideas recur, enriched each time.

## Interleaving vs Blocking

- **Blocked practice** — study one topic thoroughly, then the next (AAAA BBBB). Feels smooth and productive.
- **Interleaved practice** — mix related topics (ABAB) so learners must **discriminate** which approach applies. Feels harder and slower, but produces **better long-term retention and transfer** (a "desirable difficulty" — see spaced-repetition-systems). Interleave related skills once basics are established.

## Balance Structure With Flexibility

A fixed linear sequence assumes all learners are identical — but they aren't (see adaptive-and-personalized-learning). A good curriculum has a **sound backbone** (prerequisite-respecting) while allowing **adaptive routing** — remediate missing prerequisites, skip mastered material, branch by need. Structure the dependencies; personalize the path through them.

## Pitfalls (in understanding/using)

- **Ignoring prerequisites** — teaching topics before their foundations → learners lost through no fault of their own. Map dependencies first.
- **Jumping to abstract** without concrete grounding, or **complex** without building from simple (cognitive overload).
- **Teach-once-and-move-on** for deep concepts instead of **spiraling** back with more depth.
- **All blocked practice** — smoother but worse retention; interleave related skills.
- **No context** — diving into details without the big picture of why/where it fits.
- **Rigid linear** sequence for diverse learners (no remediation/skipping) — structure the backbone, adapt the path.
- Sequencing by **textbook convenience** rather than **learning dependencies**.
