# Game Design Fundamentals

Game design is engineering *experiences* through rules. Fun isn't random — it comes from well-understood principles about mechanics, motivation, and challenge.

## MDA Framework (the lens)

A game is understood on three levels, and designers and players approach them from opposite ends:
- **Mechanics** — the rules and systems (what the designer directly builds: jump, shoot, trade, the economy).
- **Dynamics** — the run-time behavior that *emerges* when players interact with the mechanics (tension, competition, cooperation, strategies).
- **Aesthetics** — the emotional responses evoked (challenge, discovery, fellowship, expression, fantasy). This is what the player actually experiences and remembers.
The designer builds **mechanics**, but the goal is the **aesthetic** experience — and dynamics are the unpredictable bridge. Small mechanic changes can cause big dynamic/aesthetic shifts, so design toward the *feeling* you want, then find mechanics that produce it.

## The Core Loop

Most games have a **core loop** — the fundamental cycle the player repeats (e.g. *explore → fight → get loot → upgrade → explore harder areas*). If the core loop isn't fun on its own, no amount of content saves the game. Make the moment-to-moment action satisfying first; the loop is what players do for hours.

## Player Motivation

Lasting engagement comes from **intrinsic** motivation (playing because it's inherently satisfying) more than **extrinsic** (rewards/points). **Self-Determination Theory** names three intrinsic needs a good game satisfies:
- **Autonomy** — meaningful choices; the player feels in control of their approach.
- **Competence** — a sense of growing mastery; clear feedback that they're getting better.
- **Relatedness** — connection to others or the world/characters.
Design for these, not just dopamine-loop rewards (which can feel hollow or manipulative — see dark-patterns).

## Flow (challenge vs skill)

**Flow** is the immersive "in the zone" state, reached when **challenge matches the player's skill**. Too hard → anxiety/frustration; too easy → boredom. Since skill grows as the player plays, difficulty must **rise with them** (progression, scaling, difficulty options). Keeping players in the flow channel is a central design job.

## Meaningful Choices

Interesting decisions are the heart of gameplay: choices with **real trade-offs and consequences**, where no option is strictly best. A "choice" between an obviously-correct option and a bad one isn't a choice. Give players decisions they'll think about.

## Why It Matters

Explains *why* games are fun and how to design that on purpose: build mechanics toward a target aesthetic (MDA), make the core loop satisfying, feed intrinsic motivation (autonomy/competence/relatedness), keep challenge in the flow channel, and offer meaningful choices. These apply beyond games too — to gamification and engaging product design (used well, not to manipulate).

## Pitfalls

- **Designing mechanics without a target feeling** — features that don't cohere into an experience.
- **A boring core loop** propped up by content.
- **Over-relying on extrinsic rewards** — grind that isn't fun (and can be manipulative).
- **Flat difficulty** — bored or frustrated players as skill diverges from challenge.
- **Fake choices** — decisions with an obvious correct answer.
- Confusing "more features" with "more fun."
