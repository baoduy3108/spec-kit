# Go-To-Market Strategy

A go-to-market (GTM) strategy is your plan for **reaching customers and winning them** — who you target, what you promise, and how you deliver that message and product to them. A great product with no GTM fails; GTM is how value reaches the market.

## Start With Who: Target Segment & ICP

You can't sell to "everyone." Define the **Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)** — the specific type of customer who has the problem acutely, can pay, and you can reach. Narrow beachhead first (dominate a niche, then expand) beats spraying broadly. Everything else (message, channel, price) flows from **who** you're serving (see positioning-basics, product-discovery).

## What: Value Proposition & Positioning

Nail the **value proposition** — the clear, compelling reason this customer should care: the problem you solve, the outcome you deliver, and why you over alternatives. **Positioning** (see positioning-basics) frames your product in the customer's mind against the competition. The message must be sharp and resonate with the ICP's actual pain, in their language.

## How: Distribution Channels & Motion

**Distribution is often the real bottleneck** — great products die from no path to customers. Choose channels where your ICP actually is (content/SEO, ads, communities, partnerships, outbound sales, marketplaces, app stores) and a **GTM motion**:
- **Product-led (PLG)** — the product sells itself via free trial/freemium, self-serve signup, and virality. Low cost per customer, scales for simple/individual-adoption products (see growth-loops-and-virality).
- **Sales-led** — a sales team drives deals. Necessary for high-price, complex, enterprise sales with long cycles (see b2b-sales-fundamentals).
- **Marketing-led** — demand gen via content/ads/brand feeds a funnel.
Most companies blend these. Match the motion to your **price and complexity**: cheap/simple → self-serve; expensive/complex → sales.

## Alignment Is Everything

The pieces must fit: a **low-touch price** needs a **self-serve motion** and scalable **channels**; an **enterprise price** justifies a **sales team**. A mismatch (e.g. a $20/mo product with an expensive field sales force, or a $200k product with only self-serve signup) breaks the economics (see saas-metrics — CAC must be far below LTV). Message, price, channel, and motion should reinforce each other.

## Launch & Iterate

A launch is a moment, not the strategy. Plan the launch (audience, channels, timing, assets), but treat GTM as **iterative** — measure what channels convert, refine the ICP and message with real data, double down on what works, cut what doesn't.

## Pitfalls (in understanding/using)

- **No distribution plan** — assuming a good product will be found (it won't); channels are the hard part.
- Targeting **everyone** → resonating with no one; pick a sharp ICP/beachhead.
- **Mismatched motion and price** (enterprise sales for a cheap product, or self-serve for a complex expensive one) → broken unit economics.
- Weak/vague **value proposition** that doesn't tie to real ICP pain.
- Copying a competitor's GTM without their context (audience, price, resources).
- Treating **launch = done** instead of iterating on channel/message with data.
- Ignoring **CAC vs LTV** — a channel that costs more than customers are worth isn't a strategy.
