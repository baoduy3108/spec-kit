# Stakeholder Management

Stakeholders are everyone with an interest in or influence over your work — sponsors, users, teammates, other departments, leadership, customers. Managing them well (keeping the right people informed, aligned, and supportive) is often what determines whether a project succeeds organizationally, even when the work itself is sound.

## Identify the Stakeholders

First, list **everyone affected by or able to affect** the outcome — it's usually more people than you think (the obvious sponsor, but also downstream teams, support, security, users, that one influential skeptic). Missing a key stakeholder until late is a classic cause of derailment ("nobody told legal/security"). Cast a wide net, then prioritize.

## Map by Power and Interest

A **power/interest grid** helps you spend attention wisely:
- **High power, high interest** — **manage closely**; engage deeply, they're key to success.
- **High power, low interest** — **keep satisfied**; brief them enough that they stay supportive (or at least not blockers).
- **Low power, high interest** — **keep informed**; they care and can be allies/advocates.
- **Low power, low interest** — **monitor**; light touch.
This prevents both over-communicating to everyone and neglecting someone influential.

## Tailor Communication

Different stakeholders need different things: an executive wants outcomes, risks, and the ask in two minutes; an engineer wants technical detail; a user wants to know how it affects them. **Match the message, depth, and channel to each audience** (see communicating-concisely, presentations-and-storytelling). Communicate **proactively and regularly** — surprises (especially bad ones) destroy trust; early, honest updates build it.

## Align, Build Trust, Manage Expectations

- **Understand their goals and concerns** — what does each stakeholder need/fear? Address it. People support what accounts for their interests.
- **Build trust** through reliability and transparency — do what you said, flag problems early, be honest about trade-offs.
- **Manage expectations** explicitly — agree on scope, timelines, and what "done" means up front; under-promise and over-deliver rather than the reverse.
- **Get buy-in early** — involve key stakeholders in decisions so they're co-owners, not surprised recipients.

## Handling Conflicting Priorities

Stakeholders will want different, sometimes opposing things. Surface the conflict openly, make trade-offs explicit (see roadmap-prioritization, decision-making-frameworks), tie decisions to shared goals, and, where needed, escalate to the right decision-maker. Find win-wins where possible; when you can't, be transparent about why a choice was made.

## Pitfalls (in understanding/using)

- **Missing a key stakeholder** until late → surprise objections that derail work.
- **Communicating the same way to everyone** — tailor to each audience's needs.
- **Surprising** stakeholders with bad news late — proactive early updates preserve trust.
- **Over-promising** to please, then disappointing — manage expectations honestly.
- Treating it as **manipulation** rather than genuine alignment — people sense it; build real trust.
- Ignoring the **quiet high-power** stakeholder who can quietly veto you.
- Not **re-engaging** as the project and stakeholders' priorities evolve.
