# Emotional Intelligence

Emotional intelligence (EQ) is the ability to recognize and manage your own emotions and to understand and influence others'. It often matters more than raw intellect for working with people, and unlike IQ it can be developed.

## The Four Components

- **Self-awareness** — noticing what you're feeling *as* you feel it, and how it affects your behavior and judgment. You can't manage an emotion you haven't noticed. Name it ("I'm frustrated / anxious") — labeling an emotion reduces its grip.
- **Self-regulation** — managing your reactions rather than being hijacked by them. Not suppressing feelings, but choosing your response. The core move: **pause before reacting** — a few seconds between trigger and response prevents the regretted email, the snapped reply. When flooded, take a break.
- **Empathy** — sensing what others feel and seeing their perspective. Read tone, body language, and what's *not* said. Ask yourself "what might they be feeling, and why?" before judging their behavior.
- **Social skill** — using the above to build rapport, communicate, resolve conflict, and influence — the applied output of the other three.

## In Practice

- **Pause under pressure** — the gap between stimulus and response is where EQ lives. Breathe, don't fire back while emotional (the single most useful habit).
- **Respond to the emotion, not just the words** — someone "asking about the deadline" may really be anxious about their workload; addressing the feeling often resolves the surface issue. In conflict and feedback, feeling **heard** de-escalates people (see conflict-resolution).
- **Manage your triggers** — know what sets you off and plan for it; don't make decisions or send messages while angry.
- **Assume good intent** — most annoying behavior comes from pressure or misunderstanding, not malice; this reframe alone defuses a lot.
- **Read the room** — match your approach to others' emotional state; the same message lands differently on a stressed vs relaxed person.
- **Regulate, then engage** — settle yourself first; you can't help others through emotion while flooded yourself.

## Why It Matters

EQ underpins nearly every people skill in this library — feedback, conflict resolution, negotiation, mentoring, meetings, leadership. The technically brilliant person who can't read a room or manage their reactions is limited; the one who can navigate emotions builds trust and gets things done through others. It's learnable through attention and practice.

## Pitfalls / Notes

- **Reacting instantly** to emotional triggers → regret (learn the pause).
- **Ignoring your own emotional state** while it drives your behavior.
- **Hearing only the words**, missing the feeling underneath.
- **Suppressing vs regulating** — bottling up isn't EQ; choosing your response is.
- **Assuming malice** where there's pressure or misunderstanding.
- Confusing empathy with agreement — you can understand someone's feelings without conceding the point.
