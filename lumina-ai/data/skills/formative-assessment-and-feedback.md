# Formative Assessment & Feedback

Assessment isn't just grading — done well, it's one of the most powerful tools for **learning**. **Formative** assessment checks understanding *during* learning so you (and the learner) can adjust, and **feedback** is how you turn those checks into improvement. Together they're central to tutoring (see intelligent-tutoring-systems, adaptive-and-personalized-learning).

## Formative vs Summative

- **Summative assessment** — evaluates learning **at the end** (a final exam, a grade). It measures; it doesn't help the learner improve *now* (it's too late).
- **Formative assessment** — checks understanding **during** learning (quick questions, problems, explanations, low-stakes quizzes) to reveal where the learner is, so both the learner and the teacher/system can **adapt** before moving on. "Assessment *for* learning," not just *of* learning.
Formative assessment feeds the adapt loop (see adaptive-and-personalized-learning) and doubles as retrieval practice (see retrieval-practice-and-testing-effect).

## What Makes Feedback Effective

Feedback is only as good as its qualities — bad feedback doesn't help (or hurts):
- **Timely** — as soon as possible after the attempt, while it's fresh and the learner can act on it (delayed feedback loses much of its value; see intelligent-tutoring-systems' step-level feedback).
- **Specific and actionable** — "your third step used the wrong formula because..." beats "wrong" or "good job." Vague feedback ("try harder," a bare score) gives nothing to act on. Say *what* and *how to fix/improve*.
- **Task-focused, not ego-focused** — feedback about **the work** ("this argument needs evidence") helps; feedback about **the person** ("you're smart"/"you're bad at this") can harm motivation and learning (see learner-motivation-and-engagement). Praise process/effort/strategy, not fixed ability.
- **Balanced and forward-looking** — acknowledge what's right, target what to improve, and point to the next step. Not a pile of criticism.
- **Not overwhelming** — a few high-impact points, not every error at once (cognitive load — see cognitive-load-theory).

## Close the Loop

Feedback only works if the learner **acts on it**. Give them the opportunity and expectation to **use** the feedback — revise, retry, apply it to the next problem. Feedback the learner reads and forgets changes nothing; feedback they **apply** drives improvement. This is the "feedback loop": assess → feedback → learner acts → reassess.

## Self- and Peer-Assessment

Teaching learners to **assess their own** work (against clear criteria/rubrics) builds metacognition (see learning-how-to-learn) and independence — arguably the ultimate goal (they become their own tutor). Peer feedback, done with structure, also helps both giver and receiver.

## Pitfalls (in understanding/using)

- **Only summative** assessment (grading at the end) — measures without helping learners improve in time.
- **Vague feedback** ("wrong," a bare score, "try harder") — nothing actionable.
- **Delayed** feedback — loses impact; give it while the learner can still act.
- **Ego-focused** feedback ("you're smart"/"you're bad at this") — harms motivation and a growth mindset; focus on the task/process.
- **Feedback overload** — too many points at once; prioritize the highest-impact few.
- **Not closing the loop** — feedback nobody acts on; build in revision/retry.
- Using assessment purely to **rank/grade** rather than to **guide learning and adapt**.
